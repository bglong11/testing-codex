## 1. INTRODUCTION

The Natarbora Refinery and Petrochemical Complex, part of a co-located larger South Coast Energy Development in Timor Leste, is the subject of this Preliminary Environmental and Social Impact Assessment (ESIA).

## 1.1 Natarbora Refinery and Petrochemical Complex

The Natarbora Refinery and Petrochemical Complex is a planned industrial facility to be located in Natarbora, Timor Leste, as part of the South Coast Development Plan. The Project aims to establish an optimized process configuration for integrated refining and petrochemical operations, with provisions for future expansion, and following international standards and best practices for design and operation. The complex is to be about 60 km west of Beaço on Timor-Leste's south coast, and 75 km southeast of Dili, a site selected for its favorable marine conditions that support infrastructure developments  that  will  include  jetties,  pipelines,  and  berthing  areas.  The  site  is accessible via existing road infrastructure, though improvements to road conditions will be desirable to improve construction access.

The  Natarbora  Refinery  and  Petrochemical  Complex,  designed  as  a  self-sustainable integrated facility, is part of a larger complex comprising three main areas:

- Marine Facility/Seaport
- Refinery and Petrochemical Complex
- LNG Plant with supplementary gas processing.

The integration between the refinery and petrochemical areas includes shared tankage facilities,  though  initial  assessments  suggest  the  tank  area  may  be  relatively  small compared to the process unit requirements (KBR 2022). Although this Preliminary ESIA does not cover the LNG or Seaport, it is important to recognize that assessing risks and impacts  is  challenging  without  considering  these  closely  linked  facilities,  as  each depends on the others for existence.

## 1.2 Preliminary Environmental and Socioeconomic Impact Assessment

This Preliminary Environmental and Socioeconomic Impact Assessment is compiled to evaluate the environmental and social implications of the proposed Natarbora Refinery and  Petrochemical  Complex.  The  ESIA  framework  is  designed  to  evaluate  potential impacts across  multiple  domains  including  physical  environment,  biodiversity,  social values, and economic factors, and to establish environmental management plans for project implementation.

The objectives of this Preliminary ESIA are to provide financial institutions with project risk and impact information, and to scope and plan for additional, detailed investigations and planning studies in support of project implementation.

## 2. PROJECT DESCRIPTION

The  project  description  is  limited  to  the  refinery  and  petrochemical  portions  of  the development.

## 2.1 Purpose and Potential Impacts of Oil Refinery and Petrochemical Complex

The Oil Refinery and Petrochemical Complex will refine crude oil into various products including gasoline, diesel, jet fuel, and petrochemicals, for domestic use and export sale. The complex will be implemented in three stages, beginning with an initial production capacity  based  on  a  feedstock  input  of  30,000  barrels  per  day  (bpd),  followed  by expansion to 60,000 bpd in Stage 2, and ultimately reaching 100,000 bpd in Stage 3.

The Stage 1 oil refinery is considered relatively small compared to global standards but still contains several integral process units and elements needed to convert crude oil into various  refined  products.  In  the  summary  description  below,  capacity  units  are  in thousand barrels per stream day (kBPSD) and tonnes per day (TPD).

The refinery-petrochemical facilities and the entire complex will be powered  by  electricity  generated  onsite  using  hydrocarbon  fuels, primarily derived from the hydrocarbons being processed at the site. The  maximum  case  net  energy  consumption  is  estimated  to  be 3,032,226 Gigacalories per year (Gcal/yr).

Estimated  quantities  of  produced  crude  oil,  condensate,  natural gas, and of externally sourced feedstocks such as naphtha are not as yet available, beyond the guideline quantities provided above at the beginning of the Project description. Back-calculating from the above estimated energy consumption would not be meaningful at this time.

Approximately 1,000 tonnes per hour (TPH) of makeup water will be required to operate the refinery-petrochemical facilities (or about 280  l/s).  The  water  sources  have  yet  to  be  defined,  though  it  is expected a large portion will be groundwater from deep boreholes. If the facilities operate continuously for a total 8,000 hours in a year, that represents 8 million cubic meters of freshwater consumption.

As indicated by the scale of resource use and consumption noted above, oil refinerypetrochemical facilities can have substantial environmental and social impacts due to the nature and scale of their operations. The types and nature of likely environmental impacts are briefly identified and described below.

Air Emissions include pollutants such as sulfur oxides (SOx/SO₂), nitrogen oxides (NOx), volatile organic compounds (VOCs), and particulate matter (PM). The major sources of emissions are various flue gases vented into the atmosphere, which in addition to those noted above include major volumes of carbon dioxide (CO 2 ) and carbon monoxide (CO). Flue gases result from the combustion of gas and oil in gas turbines, boilers, engines, and process heaters.

Noise Emissions. Operational noise from production and materials handling machinery and support activities such as transport and maintenance can disturb local communities and wildlife. The Project equipment designs and installations will be engineered to meet the design maximum daytime and night-time sound overpressure levels at the facility fence line, as defined by local / national / international standards.  Typically, this is 70 dB(A) Leq (i.e., 70 dB(A) equivalent continuous sound level).

Effluent Discharges. To  control risks of water pollution, all wastewaters contaminated with hydrocarbons, metals, and other elements and compounds, prior to discharge into surface water bodies, will be treated in an industrial wastewater treatment plant (IWTP) designed to international standards.

Soil Contamination. The potential for leaks and/or spills of crude oil, refined products, and  specialized  production  or  maintenance  chemicals  or  reagents  will  always  exist. Contaminated  soil  and  absorbent  materials  will  be  handled  as  hazardous  waste  (as detailed below).

Waste  Generation .  Production  of  toxic  and  hazardous  solid,  semi-solid,  and  liquid wastes, including sludge from effluent treatment plants and spent catalysts and caustic, will be managed (collected, stored, and shipped for safe disposal) as hazardous waste.

Biodiversity Loss. The site development plans will disturb local ecosystems, with likely the greater effects due to the LNG complex and seaport adjacent to the north, although these  are  not  directly  addressed  in  this  assessment  at  this  time.  These  and  related impacts are a major focus of this Preliminary ESIA.

Social Impacts. Effects on the local population, economy and livelihoods, and culture will  be  wide-ranging  and also site-specific. Among the most critical concerns are the impacts of physical and economic displacement, which could disrupt residences and traditional agricultural practices. An initial site assessment reveals that the refinery will be located approximately one kilometer from Aubeon Village, suggesting that the extent of physical displacement may be minimal. However, this assessment is preliminary, and a more detailed analysis will be needed to confirm these findings. This will require the collection of detailed and spatially-registered information about the region surrounding the project site.

This Preliminary ESIA provides a general assessment of the project's potential effects on local livelihoods, the impact of both temporary and permanent in-migration, and mid- to long-term economic opportunities. The introduction of substantial economic activity into a region with minimal industrial development and limited activities beyond agriculture and some tourism is likely to yield many positive impacts. However, while the project may create  job  opportunities  and  spur  economic  development,  it  is  anticipated  that  the distribution  of  benefits  and  risks  may  be  uneven,  potentially  leading  to  both  real  and perceived inequalities.

## 2.2 Project Site

The overall Project site encompasses approximately 1,050 hectares for main facilities, with an additional 100 hectares allocated for offices and services. A separate area of 292 hectares has been designated for a 'New Village' residential development (referred to as 'township' in some documents). The site demonstrates favorable conditions for marine infrastructure development, particularly in having reduced wave heights in the dominant range, so as to provide a more stable environment for marine facilities including jetties, pipelines, and berthing areas (HR Wallingford 2020).

## 2.3 Support Facilities

The Project incorporates various support facilities centered around utility systems and auxiliary  facilities.  The  core  infrastructure  includes  power  generation  (using  fuel)  and distribution  systems,  with  emergency  backup  power,  along  with  utilities  such  as instrument  air  compression,  raw  water  treatment,  and  cooling  systems  (a  sea  water

<!-- image -->

cooling  water  intake  and  return  system  will  be  constructed  as  part  of  the  project). Administrative  and  safety  facilities  include  a  fire  station,  administration  house,  and maintenance  workshops  to  ensure  operational  efficiency  and  emergency  response capabilities.

The facility infrastructure network encompasses internal roads, stormwater management  systems,  water  supply  facilities  (likely  sourced  from  boreholes),  and wastewater treatment systems. These systems are integrated with storage facilities and auxiliary  units  designed  to  support  operational  requirements  and  personnel  needs, including dedicated areas for chemical receiving and product storage.

## 2.4 Schedule and Phasing

The construction phase is estimated at 6 years, though KBR's assessment suggests this timeline may be conservative for the simplified refinery configuration. Town development is structured in three phases  to align with the phases  of refinery operations commissioning.

## 2.5 Project Category

The International Finance Corporation (IFC), part of the World Bank Group, employs a categorization system to evaluate and manage the potential environmental and social impacts of projects under consideration for investment. These categories guide the level of review and management needed to address project impacts effectively.

Greencorp recognizes that the impacts of the Oil Refinery and Petrochemical Complex, excluding biodiversity concerns, are expected to be largely contained within the project site and its immediate vicinity. The scale and scope of potential environmental and social impacts  are  limited,  with  the  facility's  design  incorporating  measures  to  minimize emissions,  effluents,  and  discharges.  Notably,  no  continuous  gas  flaring  is  planned during operations (KBR 2020). The site is sufficiently distant from residential settlements to limit direct effects on the local community.

Despite these considerations, Greencorp concurs that the Project should be classified as an IFC Category A project, primarily due to its location in likely critical habitat. Projects in  Category  A  are  identified  as  having  significant  environmental  impacts,  thereby requiring a full-fledged Environmental and Social Impact Assessment (ESIA) process.

## 4.1 Climate and Meteorology

The climate of Timor-Leste is predominantly influenced by the Asian tropical monsoon system,  shaped  by  the  country's  topography  and  geographic  location.  The  climate features two primary seasons: the wet season, spanning December to May, with peak rainfall typically occurring in January, February, or March and varying by region; and the dry  season,  from  June  to  November,  with  September  and  October  being  the  driest months, depending on locality. Temperatures are much less variable than rainfall, though obviously also lower at higher elevations than near sea level.

## Temperature

In  a  near-Equatorial  wet-and-dry  climate,  temperature  variability  is  less,  and  less important, that patterns of rainfall. In general, diurnal temperature variation can be more important than monthly or even annual variability. ## Rainfall

Rainfall in Timor-Leste is both high in terms of annual totals, typical of the Equatorial tropics, and also highly variable both seasonally and in accordance with topographic elevation, as is normal for the Lesser Sundas-Nusa Tenggara region. The southern coastal plain around the Natarbora site is considerably drier than the mountainous areas to the north. Recent data show widely variable total annual rainfall can range from a minimum around 740 mm to a maximum up to around 2,300 mm. Coastal regions generally receive lower annual rainfall, especially on the southern coast where Natarbora is located.

Natarbora receives annual average rainfall between 740 to 900 mm. Inland mountainous regions  receive  the  highest  amounts  of  annual  rainfall,  as  elevated  terrain  forces  air upwards, with cooling, condensation, and increased rainfall. Overall, the annual rainfall pattern in Timor-Leste is heavily influenced by topography. The central highlands barrier causes orographic lift and significantly higher rainfall than on the coastal plains.

## 4.2 Geology and Landforms

Topography

Timor-Leste occupies the eastern half of Timor Island and includes the island of Atauro and the Oecussi Enclave (surrounded by Indonesia's East Nusa Tenggara Province-not depicted on maps in this Chapter), covering approximately 15,000 km². The country's topography is predominantly mountainous, featuring rugged terrain and narrow valleys, with about 44% of the land having a slope greater than 40%. Many peaks exceed 2,000 meters, with Mount Ramelau being the highest at 2,963 meters (Figure 4-8). The mean elevation is 470 meters.

As Timor-Leste is characterized by steep mountainous terrain, landslides triggered by rainfall are a common occurrence. Intense rainfall during the wet season, combined with lack of dense vegetation in most areas, rapidly saturates the soil, increasing the risk of landslides.

## Geology

Timor-Leste's width north to south ranges from 75 to 100 km, with uplifted coral reefs and karst topography along the northeast coast. Geologically, the region is complex due to tectonic interactions between the Indo-Australian and Eurasian Plates, characterized by uplifted basement rocks and younger sediments in structurally controlled basins. An NGO study in Natarbora, the Al-Com Natural Resource Management Survey (Al-Com 2023) provides some information on the residents of the Natarbora region. Late in the Indonesian  period  (1990s),  Aubeon  was  a  transmigration  settlement,  with  people resettled there from areas identified (by the Al-Com 2023 study) as Manehat, Laclubar, and Soibada, with most were from elsewhere in Barique-Natarbora.

The  Aubeon  community  is  considered  unique  in  Timor-Leste  for  conserving  and protecting  mangrove  and  coastal  wetlands  without  fencing,  through  the  community

bylaw Tara-bandu. The Aubeon Cooperative Diakliu has established three community groups  consisting  of  fisheries  group,  livestock  group,  and  agriculture  group  and responsible for managing the restoration of over 580 ha of mangrove ecosystem. In recent years,  UNDP  has  provided  the  community  with  financial  and  technical  support, materials, and training designed to enhance the livelihoods of these three groups with the objective of creating job opportunities, reducing the unemployment rate in the Aubeon Village, and generating family income.

At the time of an earlier report, the livestock 'fattening' group--which prepares shelter and  food  for  cattle  and  buffaloes--had  already  commenced  working  on  7  ha  of  land provided  by  their  suco. ## 5.3 Economy and Infrastructure

The  Project  area's  economic  structure  appears  to  be  diverse,  with  active  sectors including  transportation,  services,  and  infrastructure  development.  As  the  previous subsection  made  clear,  the  key  primary  sectors  are  agriculture  (largely  corn/maize cultivation), animal husbandry, and fisheries including small-scale aquaculture.

The study found, of its surveyed respondents in four villages, 83% owned the land they resided  on.  Those  who  did  not  virtually  all  cultivated  land  owned  by  relatives.  Of  the landowners,  20%  reported  inheriting  the  land,  17-29%  obtained  their  land  from  the Indonesian government, 13-17% from the Timor-Leste government.  In Aubeon, virtually all landowners held land title certificates issued by the Timor-Leste government.

In the villages studied by Al-Com, 31% of farmers hired tractors to cultivate their land, mostly for rice cultivation. The tractors, provided by an NGO, are breaking down, and access to them is diminishing, so that rice growing has been declining since 2004. About 69% of Aubeon farmers plant some wet (paddy) rice, mostly  (88-90%) only one crop annually, and virtually none of the rice is sold. Aubeon had the most rice cultivation of the villages studied, likely due to its transmigration history and being entirely on level coastal plain land. Constricted water supply is another problem with rice farming.

Most farmers use only hand labor. Slash-and-burn is used by 16% of farmers. The top three crops planted by respondents were corn/maize (33%), cassava (17%), and banana (11%). Soybean, mung bean, and long bean are also grown. Only 41% of landowners in the study sold any crops, so it can be assumed the community is largely subsistence farmers. Very small percentages use fertilizer, pesticides, and herbicides. Essentially all households keep some cattle, buffalo, or other livestock. Free-grazing animals seem to reduce  crop  yields,  and  farmers  lack  funds  to  erect  fences.  Many  residents  hunt  to supplement  their  diet  and  income.  Importantly,  some  58%  of  landowners  in  Aubeon report owning some teak trees, though numbers and areas involved seem to be limited.

<!-- image -->

Very little teak is sold, and it may function mostly as a store of household value. No other tree crops are grown in quantities beyond subsistence levels.

Land rights and use in Aubeon are reported as 'contentious,' as many farmers previously rented land for rice growing from traditional landowners, who have since withdrawn their leases to rent the land to a Chinese agricultural company. Those farmers have turned to corn and livestock. Some work as laborers for the Chinese company.

The area's infrastructure includes basic utilities such as water supply facilities, sewerage and  wastewater  treatment,  and  power  generation  facilities.  Social  infrastructure comprises assembly halls, recreation facilities, libraries, and public services including security  offices  and  fire  departments.  Limited  information  on  these  factors  has  been obtained to date.

## 6. STAKEHOLDER ENGAGEMENT

Stakeholder  engagement  is  the  basis  for building the strong, constructive,  and responsive  relationships  with  Project-Affected  Communities  essential  for  successful management of a development's environmental and social impacts. The character of approach, frequency, and level of effort of stakeholder engagement must be appropriate for the culture in which the project is operating, and these factors vary during the project's phased development. Above all, the program must be commensurate with the project's risks and adverse impacts.

In  Timor-Leste,  Decree-Law  No.  5/2011  on  Environmental  Licensing  mandates  public consultation and transparency in environmental permitting. Public input is considered integral to the licensing process to ensure community concerns and traditional rights are respected. Project documents and decisions are by law accessible to the public. The Law mandates Impact and Benefits Agreements (IBA) to address compensation and rights for affected communities.

Available documentation indicates community disclosure and consultation and overall stakeholder engagement for the South Coast hydrocarbons development date back more than 10 years. Logically, local residents can reasonably be expected to be sceptical about project information they are provided, when to date essentially none of the developments have actually transpired.

This Chapter, absent any available information about recent stakeholder engagement activities at the Natarbora  Site, presents  briefly information on the disclosure, engagement, and resettlement planning actions taken at Suai Supply Base, a related South Coast project located 125 km southeast of the Natarbora Refinery site, from about 2012 through 2015. This is considered to indicate how Timor Gap would approach these challenges at Natarbora. These prior activities show the proponent's intention to comply with international standards of practice in the all-important stakeholder engagement and management activities, which will also be discussed in this Chapter.

Stakeholder  engagement  is  an  ongoing  process  that  may  involve,  as  appropriate: stakeholder  analysis  and  planning,  disclosure  and  dissemination  of  information, consultation  and  participation,  grievance  mechanism,  and  ongoing  reporting  and continued engagement.

## Greenhous Gas Emissions

Oil  refineries  are  significant  sources  of  greenhouse  gas  (GHG)  emissions  due  to  the various processes involved in refining crude oil into usable products. The primary GHG emissions from refineries include carbon dioxide (CO 2 ), methane (CH 4 ), and nitrous oxide (N2 O).

Refineries  use  large  amounts  of  energy,  primarily  from  burning  fossil  fuels  to  power heaters, boilers, and other process equipment. Fuel combustion results in substantial CO2 emissions. Various refining processes, such as catalytic cracking and hydrotreating, can release both CO 2 and CH4 (methane) as by-products of chemical reactions. Fugitive emissions caused by equipment leaks and maintenance activities are another concern.

As stated elsewhere, the complex is being designed to avoid the need for continuous flaring  of  gas,  and  standard  good  practice  for  flare  monitoring  will  be  applied.  The financial  model  assumes  non-turnaround  year  plant  availability  above  96%,  which should minimize unplanned venting and flaring of gas and associated GHG emissions.

In sum, releases of carbon dioxide (CO₂) and methane (CH₄) from the complex, in tonnes of carbon dioxide equivalent (CO 2-Eq ), are estimated at 2.072 million tonnes per year (TPY), consisting  of  300,000  TGY  for  the  refinery  and  1,772,000  TPY  for  the  petrochemical complex.

<!-- image -->

Under  the  IFC  Performance  Standards,  specifically  Performance  Standard  3  (PS3), projects are required to manage and report their greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions if the project  emits  more  than  25,000  metric  tons  of  CO 2 -equivalent  per  year.  As  such  the Natabora Oil Refinery and Petrochemical Complex Projects is encouraged to quantify and report their emissions annually, as well as consider options for reducing emissions where feasible.

## Noise Emissions

During  construction,  construction  activities  will  contribute  to  the  noise  environment, especially during operations of heavy machinery and metalwork. Noise generation will be intermittent and limited to the construction period, estimated to last 3 years.

During operation, noise can be generated from several sources. Pumps, compressors and  turbines  often  run  continuously  and  produce  noise  levels.  Fans  and  other mechanical components used in heat exchangers and cooling towers often generate considerable  noise.  In  addition,  generation  and  movement  of  steam,  along  with  the operation of associated turbines, can be a significant noise source.

Operations that lend themselves to effective noise management  often involve engineering controls, such as equipment enclosures, sound dampening materials, and regular maintenance to prevent abnormal noise from worn or unbalanced equipment. Equipment design and location will be developed to meet the design maximum noise level  at  the  facility  fence  line  defined  by  local  /  national  /  international  standards, generally 70 dB(A) Leq (i.e. 70 dB(A) equivalent continuous sound level.

In addition, the oil refinery is distant from the nearest communities, and noise impacts on surrounding communities are likely to be minimal.

## 7.3 Water Resources

The Project area is characterized by a seasonally water-constrained and thus sensitive hydrological  environment,  with  the  presence  of  groundwater  springs  and  drainage channels that require careful management. Project development includes infrastructure for stormwater management, water supply facilities, and wastewater treatment systems to minimize environmental impacts. A dual water supply system is planned, utilizing both freshwater and seawater, with seawater used preliminary for cooling water purposes.

## Freshwater

The  proposed  Natarbora  Refinery  and  Petrochemical  Complex  requires  a  significant amount  of  freshwater  for  operations.  However,  on  the  south  coast  of  Timor-Leste, freshwater availability during the dry season is limited due reduced rainfall. The south coast, like much of the country, receives minimal rainfall during the dry season. Given the relatively small catchment area size, rivers and streams on the south coast are highly seasonal,  with  flow  volumes  diminishing  significantly  during  the  dry  months;  smaller streams usually dry up entirely.

The  Project  considers  groundwater  as  an  important  source  of  freshwater.  Access  to groundwater  is  based  on  geological  formations  and  depth  of  aquifers,  with  limited information available on both factors available at the time of this Preliminary ESIA.

<!-- image -->

<!-- image -->

The  significance  of  the  groundwater  extraction  rate  of  280  liters  per  second  (l/s)  as proposed in this Project depends on the capacity and sustainability of the local aquifer system (which is largely unknown), local water demand, and environmental considerations. Only if the aquifer system has a large capacity, and a high recharge rate, could extracting 280 l/s be sustainable.

The  typical  yield  of  industrial  deep  wells  varies  widely,  depending  on  geological characteristics of the aquifer, the depth of the well, and local hydrogeological conditions, and can range from 5 to 50 liters per second (l/s). To support the project, groundwater development using multiple wells will be required. To determine if this extraction rate is sustainable, it will be important to conduct further hydrogeological studies.

## Sea Water

The sea water cooling system duty load is 620 MW, assumed as requiring up to 107,000 cubic meters per hour (m 3 /hr or TPH) of water (or about 30 m 3 /s) with a maximum sea water temperature rise of 5°C within the mixing zone.

The design of the seawater cooling system will consider several measures to minimize impact on the local ecosystem. The outfall will be designed to promote rapid mixing and dilution of the warmer water. Appropriate screens installed on intake structures assist in reducing entrainment (organisms drawn into the system) and impingement (organisms trapped against intake screens). The use of chemicals in the cooling process, such as anti-fouling agents or biocides, will be managed to avoid harmful concentrations in the discharge.  To  optimize  the  location  and  design  of  the  intake  and  outfall  structure, oceanographic modelling is recommended.

## Effluent Discharges

All  wastewaters  contaminated  with  hydrocarbons,  metals,  and  other  elements  and compounds, prior to discharge into surface water bodies, will be treated in an industrial wastewater treatment plant (IWTP) designed to international standards. The IWTP will probably incorporate primary, secondary, and tertiary treatment stages. The treatment process train, which is in design, will ensure key water quality parameters (dissolved and suspended metals and other solids, pH, biochemical oxygen demand (BOD), chemical oxygen  demand  (COD),  and  petroleum  hydrocarbons)  will  not  exceed  national  and international  threshold  limit  standards  before  the  treated  water  is  released  to  the environment.

## 7.5 Ecology and Biodiversity

The proposed refinery development at Natarbora presents several challenges regarding coastal  and  marine  ecosystems.  The  presence  of  extensive  and  relatively  healthy mangrove areas on and adjacent to the selected site will require carefully adapting the facility design, not only to preserve existing biodiversity and ecological services inherent

in  the  mangrove,  but  also  to  benefit  from  the  coastal  protective  functions  of  the ecosystem (Wallingford 2020). The previous site characterization highlighted the need for careful  consideration of marine environment stability, particularly with respect to  the refinery's waterfront infrastructure development impacts on natural coastal processes.

In common with previous Project documentation, this Preliminary ESIA acknowledges the need for comprehensive biodiversity assessment to plan conservation strategies. In considering the efforts already made by the Aubeon Cooperative Diakliu, there are two unavoidable outcomes (among others) of this Preliminary ESIA:

- Designation of Aubeon Lagoon (and possibly a more extensive area) as Critical Habitat is indicated.
- Compilation of a full-fledged ESIA for the Natarbora Refinery and Petrochemical Complex, supported by a Biodiversity Action Plan is necessary.

Detailed field  surveys of species-level  data  specific  to  this  project  have  not  yet  been conducted, and are required given the site's location within a Key Biodiversity Area and likely Critical Habitat. Such data collection will support assessing potential impacts and will inform effective mitigation strategies.

Design considerations have previously highlighted the importance of conserving existing mangrove ecosystems and the potential for creating new ones, as outlined by Wallingford in  2020.  To  ensure  that  biodiversity  considerations  are  adequately  addressed,  the rationale for the site selection of the entire complex-including the refinery, petrochemical facilities,  LNG  plant,  and  port-must  be  thoroughly  documented.  This documentation  should  clearly  demonstrate  how  the  preservation  of  biodiversity  has been integrated into the decision-making process for site selection.

## 7.6 Socioeconomic Impacts

The  Natarbora  Refinery  Project  and  the  broader  South  Coast  Project  represent  a significant economic development initiative with far-reaching socioeconomic implications  for  Timor-Leste.  Analysis  suggests  potential  impacts  across  multiple economic  sectors,  with  a  focus  on  local  employment,  business  development,  and macroeconomic  effects.  To  assess  the  economy-wide  impacts  of  these  projects, economic modeling approaches such as input-output (I-O) multipliers and computable general equilibrium (CGE) modeling have been employed.

The  analysis  predicts  both  direct  and  indirect  effects  on  employment,  income,  and production, highlighting the importance of managing social and economic infrastructure, property management, labor supply, and local business facilitation. Key considerations include the potential for cost inflation and impacts on trade competitiveness, which may affect traditional economic activities.

<!-- image -->

Demographic information from Chapter 5 indicates that Timor-Leste's population and development  are  primarily  concentrated  on  the  north  coast  near  Dili,  which  itself contains a quarter of the population. In contrast, Manatuto, where the project is located, comprises less than 2% of the population, with most residents situated slightly east of Dili.  This  demographic  imbalance  suggests  local  residents  may  face  challenges competing for project-related jobs, particularly in construction and operations. Aubeon, located  a  five-hour  drive  from  Dili,  may  see  this  travel  time  reduced  with  road improvements,  so  that  it  will  be  highly  unlikely  that  managerial,  supervisory,  and technical staff reside in Natarbora outside company accommodations.

Consequently, there is no assurance that residents in the vicinity of the refinery site will significantly  benefit  from  its  construction  and  operation.  Some  residents  may  face displacement from crop fields and pastures. Although the refinery site will be relocated to  minimize  mangrove  impacts,  bringing  potential  noise  and  air  emissions  nearer  to residences, road infrastructure improvements could be dominated by traffic unrelated to local  needs.  While  physical  relocation  may  not  be  necessary,  adverse  effects  on livelihoods are likely.

Some residents may secure employment within the project, though not in higher-paying roles. While increased economic activity during refinery construction and operation may spur  local  business  development,  residents  face  competition  from  better-prepared, external interests.

To  mitigate  these  impacts,  it  will  be  important  to  implement  continuous,  organized programs for stakeholder outreach, resettlement, livelihood preservation, and community development. Planning for these efforts should begin prior to construction and should be ongoing, addressing any unresolved issues and grievances from earlier consultation phases.

## 7.7 Sociocultural Impacts

The  industrialization  of  a  relatively  isolated  rural  region  will  inevitably  transform  its sociocultural  landscape,  leading  to  changes  for  which  residents  may  be  largely unprepared. Past experiences, such as those detailed in the Suai economic assessment, have  acknowledged broader social impacts, even though these were not the primary focus of the analysis.

As highlighted in Chapter 5, the country and region are characterized by a large, youthful labor  force.  While  the  development  of  the  hydrocarbon  industry  and  associated commercial  growth  offers  new  opportunities,  it  cannot  employ  everyone,  potentially exacerbating  perceived  economic  inequalities.  To  secure  a  stable  "social  license  to operate," it is crucial to foster investment in agriculture, fisheries, and tourism, which could absorb more of the labor force and distribute economic benefits more equitably.

<!-- image -->

In a generally impoverished society, focusing on economic factors is essential. However, for  those  unable  to  reap  economic  benefits,  cultural  shifts  may  provoke  unexpected reactions  and  conflicts.  Anticipating  these  outcomes  requires  a  focus  on  vulnerable groups and sensitive cultural elements. Baseline social assessments and stakeholder engagement  should  prioritize  dialogue  with  community  leaders,  including  formal, informal, religious, and traditional authorities, to identify potential threats to community stability, adaptability, and resilience. Particular attention should be given to sacred sites and culturally significant areas, and governance issues of cultural significance. Preparing for sociocultural impacts involves understanding these aspects and leveraging them to support a balanced and sustainable development process.

## 7.8 Cumulative and Residual Impacts

Cumulative  impact  analysis  will  only  be  possible  with  a  better  understanding  of  the closely associated LNG and Seaport (Marine Facility) developments that are adjacent to the refinery-petrochemical site and are in reality the same project.

## 7.9 Valued Environmental Components

Previous environmental assessments for the South Coast developments, including the comprehensive Strategic Environmental Impact Statement prepared by Worley Parsons, Timor  Gap,  and  SERN,  have  not  definitively  identified  specific  Valued  Environmental Components  (VECs)  associated  with  the  southern  coast  environment  and  energy development  sites  identified  in  the  past.  The  Pre-FEED  stage  site  characterization assessment for the Natarbora LNG facility did identify (in a preliminary analysis) several key environmental components, which were impacts on mangroves, coastal processes, wildlife, and groundwater.

There is little question (and no surprise) all four of these are considered significant issues. In this preliminary ESIA, the clearly obvious VEC is the Aubeon Mangrove Lagoon, also known  as  the  Naan  Kurun  Lagoon.  Its  reputed  natural  beauty  and  relatively  pristine condition attract visitors from as far away as Dili who are willing to walk more than an hour from the road. Some consider it among the best mangrove sites in the country. Being within  and  adjacent  to  the  Project  site,  it  will  require  design  efforts  that  allow  for coexistence. The mangrove area nearby along the estuary of the Rib (River) Sahen could also be considered.

As detailed in Chapter 3 on Environmental Baseline Conditions, the project area likely harbors  several  endangered  and  endemic  species,  highlighting  local  wildlife  as  a significant Valued Environmental Component (VEC). This underscores the importance of preserving biodiversity and ensuring that project activities do not adversely affect these critical habitats.

<!-- image -->

Groundwater  also  constitutes  an  important  VEC  due  to  the  prevailing  water  scarcity during the dry season. This scarcity, coupled with the substantial freshwater demand anticipated from the new development, requires careful management and sustainable sourcing of groundwater resources.

## 7.10 Impacts of Environment on Project

The  Natarbora  site  presents  an  array  of  environmental  conditions  that  pose  risks  to project development, particularly in the context of climate change. While these risks are not entirely new, their characteristics, frequency, and intensity are expected to evolve with changing climate patterns. The most significant climate change implications for this area include sea level rise and increased rainfall intensity.

## Sea Level Rise

Initial  assessments  indicate  that  sea  levels  near  Timor-Leste  have  been  rising  at approximately  9  mm  per  year  since  1993,  which  surpasses  the  global  average  rate. Projections under a very high emissions scenario estimate a rise of 9-18 cm by 2030 and potentially more than 1 meter by 2100. Such increases, coupled with natural variability, are likely to exacerbate the effects of storm surges and coastal flooding.

## Changes in Rainfall Patterns

Long-term projections suggest that while the overall rainfall in Timor-Leste may remain consistent with current patterns, there will likely be an uptick in extreme rainfall events. This  shift  is  expected  to  intensify  the  existing  high-risk  of  coastal  flooding  in  the  site region.

## Increased Saltwater Intrusion

Sea level rise will increase saltwater intrusion into coastal aquifers. As sea levels rise, the increased hydraulic pressure from the ocean will push seawater inland. Groundwater pumping  from  the  coastal  aquifer,  as  planned,  will  lower  the  groundwater  table, increasing the likelihood of saltwater moving inland, potentially affecting water quality in the bore field needed to support the new development.

## 8. MANAGEMENT AND MITIGATION MEASURES

The  hierarchy  of  responses  to  or  management  of  risks  and  impacts  is  generally considered  to  be,  in  order,  avoidance,  minimization,  mitigation  and  control,  and offsetting/compensation.

## 8.1 Air Quality Management and Mitigation

Project  facilities  will  be  designed  to  ensure  combustion  meeting  local  /  national  / international  standards  as  required  for  NOx  emissions,  through  selection  of  low  NOx burners and control of variation in gas quality. SOx emissions will be low, due to the very low  quantity  of  sulfur  contained  in  feed  crude  oil,  LPG,  ethane,  furnace  tail  gas,  and natural gas. As stated elsewhere, sulfur removed in the refining process will be recovered in a small Sulfur Recovery Unit. Tail Gas sulfur emissions from this recovery unit will be below 4 kg SO 2 /day.

The complex is being designed to avoid the need for continuous flaring of gas (other than flare pilots), and standard good practice for flare monitoring will be applied, to be defined in subsequent project stages. The financial model assumes non-turnaround year plant availability  above  96%,  which  should  minimize  unplanned  venting  and  flaring  of  gas. Currently two flares are planned for the complex, located at distances approximately 3 km from Aubeon Village, 3.5 km from Natarbora, and 9 km from the New Village housing complex. The nearest flare is about 1 km from the plant's office and services area.

The Project's fugitive emission monitoring and control program is still being conceptually designed. This is expected to include careful equipment selection and a leak detection and repair (LDAR) program with fugitive emission monitoring hardware and best practice inspection  procedures  for  plant  areas  associated  with  high  likelihood  of  fugitive emissions.

## 8.2 Water Pollution

To  control  risks  of  water  pollution,  all  wastewaters  contaminated  with  hydrocarbons, metals,  and  other  elements  and  compounds,  prior  to  discharge  into  surface  water bodies, will be treated in an industrial wastewater treatment plant (IWTP) designed to international  standards.  The  IWTP  will  probably  incorporate  primary,  secondary,  and tertiary treatment stages. The treatment process, which is in design, will ensure key water quality parameters (dissolved and suspended metals and other solids, pH, biochemical oxygen demand (BOD), chemical oxygen demand (COD), and petroleum hydrocarbons) will not exceed national and international threshold limit standards before the treated water is released to the environment. The quality of wastewater and treated water will be defined in the subsequent stages.

<!-- image -->

Facility drainage systems are being designed with floor drains in process areas to collect and recover leaks and spills, flowing into the IWTP. Site stormwater drainage for roofs and open  areas  will  be  designed  with  one  or  more  control  basins/lagoons  to  intercept potentially  contaminated  runoff  and  leaks  or  spills,  with  the  option  of  rerouting  such runoff into the IWTP.

## 8.3 Waste Management

Production of toxic and hazardous solid, semi-solid, and liquid wastes, including sludge from  effluent  treatment  plants  and  spent  catalysts  and  caustic,  will  be  managed (collected,  stored,  and  shipped  for  safe  disposal)  as  hazardous  waste.  All  hazardous wastes  will  be  managed  in  compliance  with  international  industry  standards.  Small quantities of such substances will be produced, with the types and amounts (likely a few tonnes per year) being estimated in design planning.

Such wastes will be collected at the points of generation, packaged in secure, purposedesigned containers (or tanks), inventoried, and stored in a designated area designed to control hazards of fire, fume releases, leaks, and spills. Periodically, hazardous wastes will  be  transported  offsite,  likely  by  road  in  control  of  licensed  transporters  and accompanied by manifest documents, for treatment at a specialist facility. This facility is likely to be in Indonesia or elsewhere outside of Timor Leste.

## 8.4 Biodiversity Impact Management and Mitigation

The project area encompasses vital coastal ecosystems, including mangrove habitats and  essential  wildlife  areas.  Key  management  strategies  focus  on  preserving  existing mangrove areas, with design considerations aimed at avoiding dense mangrove zones, which may necessitate the loss of some fringe mangrove areas. Efforts to site the refinery complex will strive to maintain a safe distance from residential areas and agricultural lands.  Unfortunately,  some  near-upland-fringe  mangrove  habitat  loss  is  anticipated, exacerbated  by  linear  barriers  from  two  pipeline  corridors  required  for  cooling  water intake  and  effluent  discharge.  To  offset  these  impacts,  initiatives  might  include community  development  programs  aimed  at  enhancing,  restoring,  and  improving mangrove conditions at nearby degraded sites, aligning with ongoing local conservation efforts. (These corridors may be co-located with port facility infrastructure also crossing the intertidal zone, to minimize such impacts.)

The area also supports other sensitive wildlife habitat, including turtle nesting beach sites and bat colonies, necessitating further documentation and specialized conservation strategies compiled within a Biodiversity Action/Management Plan (BAMP). Appropriate conservation measures  will integrate with mangrove and shoreline protection efforts, with particular attention to the layout and design of inland housing to minimize impacts on bat colonies.

<!-- image -->

The BAMP's development priorities include enhancing the Critical Habitat Assessment (CHA) and the project's biological and ecological baseline. Essential tasks include:

- Engaging  local  experts,  such  as  fishers,  hunters,  and  wildlife  collectors,  to incorporate local and indigenous knowledge for better biodiversity insights and conservation priorities.
- Defining ecological areas for detailed assessment, starting within the boundary of the project's industrial facilities and proposed New Village housing complex, and extending outward to at least a 1-km buffer. These studies should integrate with the Census/Socioeconomic Survey and the Stakeholder Engagement Plan/Program (SEP).
- Conducting field surveys for the presence of IUCN Critically Endangered (CR), Endangered (EN), and Vulnerable species within the project area and its buffers.
- Addressing  economically  and  culturally  important  species,  human-hazardous species, agricultural/livestock pests, and invasive flora and fauna in surveys.
- Analyzing survey findings to create detailed habitat maps, indicating the extent of mangroves, swamp forests, transition zones, fisheries, and critical feeding areas.
- Studying  the  impacts  of  the  New  Village,  refinery,  LNG,  and  port  complex  on habitat loss, fragmentation, and connectivity.
- Initiating Biodiversity Offset Plan (BOP) studies, beginning with reconnaissance and detailed studies of remote potential offset sites.
- Incorporating  the  region's  biodiversity  vulnerability  to  climate  change  into  the Project Climate Change Risk Assessment to develop adaptive strategies.

The  outcome  of  these  tasks  is  the  establishment  of  a  long-term  management  and monitoring program as a core component of the ESMP, aimed at tracking biodiversity and ecosystem health changes to inform adaptive management strategies. If the BAMP is confined to the refinery-petrochemical facility, all investigations should align with those conducted for the LNG and Port/Marine Facility to assess potential cumulative impacts alongside other regional developments.

## 8.5 Socioeconomic and Sociocultural Impact Management

The  Natarbora  Refinery  and  Petrochemical  Complex,  within  the  wider  South  Coast development, incorporates several socioeconomic management measures focused on maximizing benefits for the people of Timor-Leste while mitigating potential risks. Key management  priorities  include  local  employment  generation,  business  development opportunities, and infrastructure enhancement.

The Project early documents had ambitious aims to integrate unskilled and low-skilled workers from the subsistence economy into the formal sector, supported by targeted training and capacity building initiatives (TMP 2021). On the South Coast, it is expected stimulation  of  higher  levels  of  economic  activity  will  assist  primary  sector  workers

(farmers,  livestock  raisers,  and  fishers),  along  with  local  market  traders  and  service providers,  with  increased  market  demand,  improved  prices,  and  enhanced  local infrastructure and services.

Management measures emphasize the need for balanced development, addressing both immediate  project  impacts  and  longer-term  socioeconomic  transformation.  Special attention is given to managing cost inflation risks and potential impacts on traditional economic activities, while ensuring equitable distribution of Project benefits (TMP 2021). The  route  to  attaining  these  objectives  will  be  through  continuous  programs  of stakeholder  engagement  and  public  participation  from  the  earliest  planning  stages, maintained throughout the inevitably disruptive construction period, and continued in commissioning-startup and institutionalized for the operation phase.

For all appropriation of individual and communal-public land, and for usage of roads and watercourses  around  the  sites,  it  will  be  essential  to  ensure  not  only  respectful  and culturally appropriate patterns of engagement, but also to guard a perception of fairness throughout.  Grievances  must  be  resolved  quickly  and  equitably,  even  at  the  risk  of delaying critical path items.

The project's sociocultural management  framework  is  not  clearly distinct from socioeconomic management, recognizing that in a traditional and largely subsistence culture, rights to use and access lands, waters, and natural resources (and benefit from ecosystem services) are not perceived as economic assets but inherent to individual and community existence and identity. Being forced into purely transactional approaches will favor the inevitable speculators, provocateurs, and fraudsters. This is best dealt with by having  long-established  knowledge  of  the  local  cultural  dynamics  and  structures  of authority  and  influence.  There  can  be  no  substitute  for  having  long-established relationships  with  respected  figures  and  formal  and  informal  leaders  within  the communities.

## 8.6 Residual and Cumulative Impact Management

While the refinery and petrochemical complex can be viewed as an isolated industrial development  with  potential  environmental  and  social  impacts,  mitigation  strategies primarily focused on mangrove preservation and coastal protection may be insufficient when  considering  the  collective  influence  of  additional  facilities  such  as  the  gas liquefaction  (LNG)  plant  and  seaport  planned  for  adjacent  coastal  areas.  These developments  significantly  alter  the  context  of  the  Environmental  and  Social  Impact Assessment and require a holistic approach to impact management.

The key residual impacts involve potential disruptions to local community livelihoods, including farming, herding, and fishing, due to reduced access to land, coastal waters, and mangrove lagoons. Biodiversity effects may also lead to secondary impacts, such as diminished  fish  catches.  The  proposed  development  of  the  LNG  facility  and  seaport

<!-- image -->

markedly  amplifies  these  concerns,  necessitating  a  comprehensive  evaluation  of cumulative impacts.

Operational impact management allows for long-term planning, but the Construction Environmental  and  Social  Management  Plan  must  adapt  to  unpredictable,  dynamic conditions.  This  includes  managing  the  behavior  of  a  diverse  labor  force,  addressing weather-dependent scheduling, and resolving labor actions that could strain relationships with local communities. Successful management requires collaboration with local community leaders and government officials to ensure community safety and cohesion without excessively constraining the workforce.

Cumulative  impacts  extend  beyond  the  construction  phase.  Although  the  refinery's isolated  impact  on  the  village  center  and  southern  Aubeon  Mangrove  Lagoon  is significant yet manageable, the entire complex's reliance on natural gas and condensate brought ashore via the seaport favors a unified assessment. The anticipated conversion of a lagoon segment into a harbor will result in substantial ecological changes, including constructing navigation infrastructure and dockage, as well as dredging-significantly altering aquatic and coastal habitats.

Furthermore,  the  simultaneous  construction  of  the  refinery,  LNG  plant,  and  port  is essential,  given  their  interdependency.  This  requires  a  larger  workforce  and  extended construction timeline, inevitably leading to major impacts on nearby residents due to limited road access. Designing, constructing, and operating these facilities with minimal social and environmental disruption represent formidable challenges.

Residual  impacts  may  involve  significant  losses  of  mangrove  habitats  and  possibly agricultural lands, necessitating extensive offset and compensation programs. Additionally, implementing engineering solutions to buffer and protect biodiversity while preserving residents' lifestyles is important. Continuous stakeholder engagement and social and environmental monitoring are essential throughout the project's lifecycle to ensure adaptive and responsive management strategies.

## 8.7 Enhancing Benefits

The project and broader South Coast development offer substantial opportunities for economic  and  social  progress  in  Timor-Leste,  aiming  to  boost  national  GDP ,  export earnings, and government revenues. The initiative incorporates diverse benefit enhancement measures targeting macroeconomic growth alongside local community development through job creation, skills training, and infrastructure improvements.

Key strategies focus on fostering sustainable economic opportunities by promoting local employment and business development, while addressing challenges like cost inflation and  impacts  on  traditional  economic  activities.  Early  project  documents  highlight distributing benefits throughout the economy via strategic wage spending and supplier

<!-- image -->

engagement,  with  particular  emphasis  on  opportunities  for  landowners  and  social infrastructure advancement.

Successfully managing cumulative and residual impacts require a project design and operational scheme that deliver net benefits to local communities, alongside improved environmental quality. With careful planning, the complex's long-term operation should ideally strengthen local, regional, and national economies while maintaining environmental standards.

The  construction  phase  poses  a  significant  disruptive  challenge.  To  mitigate  these impacts,  a  benefit-enhancing  strategy  may  involve  designing  construction  facilitiescamps, housing, workshops, utilities, and amenities-for future use. While this approach may  be  costlier  upfront,  converting  temporary  structures  for  community  use  in administration,  education,  recreation,  or  business  can  lower  overall  lifecycle  costs, emphasizing long-term community development imperatives.

## 9. ALTERNATIVES ANALYSIS

Project  alternatives  considered  in  this  Chapter  are  no  action  option,  technology alternatives, and siting alternatives.

## 9.1 No Action Option

The  No  Action  Alternative  represents  the  continuation  of  baseline  conditions  without implementation  of  the  proposed  project  in  the  Natarbora  region.  The  No-Action Alternative  eliminates  the  possibility  that  Timor-Leste  increases  its  benefits  from development of its hydrocarbons in the immediate future. The local community and the Natarbora region in this case will not be affected by any of the adverse impacts described in this document. Neither will they experience any benefits. Cancelation or postponement of Project plans can be expected to reduce their faith and trust in their national government and in the oil and gas industry.

## 9.2 Technology Alternatives

Current project documentation indicates that the assessment of technology alternatives is  still  underway,  with  final  decisions  contingent  upon  detailed  feasibility studies  and front-end engineering design (FEED). The design strategy prioritizes flexibility, enabling the project to adapt to market demands and leverage cutting-edge technologies from licensors.  The  evaluation  encompasses  facilities  configuration,  capital  and  operating costs,  and  production  profiles,  although  specifics  remain  under  study.  Any  chosen technology for the facility's final design is likely to maintain a similar suite of inputs and outputs.  These  include  feedstocks  and  products,  as  well  as  emissions  related  to  air, noise, energy, water usage, and effluent discharge. While variations in these inputs and outputs and in technology could alter environmental impacts, such changes would incur much  the  same  nature  and  scale  of  impacts  as  described  and  assessed  in  this Preliminary ESIA.

## 9.3 Siting Alternatives

The site selection process historically concentrated on two key locations along TimorLeste's  southern  coast:  the  Natarbora  and  Beaço  sites,  approximately  60  kilometers apart (Wallingford 2020). During the Pre-FEED stage, site characterization indicated that Natarbora  offered  more  favorable  marine  conditions,  particularly  concerning  wave heights,  crucial  for  developing  marine  infrastructure  such  as  jetties,  pipelines,  and berthing areas.

However, the decision to develop port facilities within the Aubeon Lagoon's favorable oceanographic conditions brings biodiversity impacts into focus. The lagoon represents a high-quality, relatively intact mangrove ecosystem, with the surrounding area classified by the IUCN as a Key Biodiversity Area. While this does not necessarily constitute a "fatal

If  the  site  selection  remains  unchanged,  there  will  be  a  substantial  responsibility  to ensure that planning, design, construction, and operation efforts rigorously protect and preserve the environmental and ecological values of this sensitive site.

## 10. CONCLUSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS

In conclusion, the Preliminary Environmental and Social Impact Assessment (ESIA) for the Natarbora Oil Refinery and Petrochemical Complex Project underscores the potential for significant economic and social advancement in Timor-Leste, while simultaneously identifying critical environmental and sociocultural impacts that must be addressed to ensure the project's sustainable development.

The  preliminary  assessment  indicates  that  the  project  is  environmentally  feasible, contingent  upon  the  implementation  of  recommended  management  and  mitigation strategies.  Achieving  success  will  depend  significantly  on  the  outcomes  of  further environmental studies, as outlined in the following recommendations:

- Baseline Surveys: Extensive biological surveys are necessary to more accurately understand the presence and distribution of sensitive wildlife within the project area.
- Critical  Habitat  Determination:  Additional  efforts  are  required  to  ascertain  the presence of critical habitats and defining their characteristics. Identifying these areas  will  be  essential  for  prioritizing  conservation  efforts  and  aligning  with international best practices.
- Intensive field research is required to assess endangered, threatened, exploited, and economically significant terrestrial and aquatic species both in the region and at  the  project  site.  Local  villagers  have  expressed  concern  about  the  attention given  to  certain  species  that  have  become  primary  sources  of  supplemental income through hunting and the live-capture trade, which significantly contributes  to  essential  expenses  like  paying  school  fees.  Understanding  the dynamics of these species is vital not only for biodiversity conservation but also for supporting the economic well-being of the community.
- Biodiversity Action Plan: Development must be accompanied by a comprehensive Biodiversity  Action/Management  Plan  to  ensure  proactive  management  and protection of local ecosystems.
- Biodiversity Offsets: In accordance with Good International Industry Practices, particularly IFC Performance Standard 6, it is advisable to identify and implement biodiversity offsets. This will help compensate for any unavoidable impacts on biodiversity.
- Stakeholder Engagement: Further community consultation is essential to address a broad range of concerns spanning environmental, gender, socioeconomic, and cultural  issues,  as  well  as  the  protection  of  endangered  species.  Both  the indigenous and long-established settler communities in Aubeon and Natarbora expect  to  be  actively  involved  in  discussions  about  the  short-  and  long-term impacts of the project. They are acutely aware of the potential for most project jobs  to  be  filled  by  individuals  from  outside  the  district,  or  even  by  foreigners.

<!-- image -->

Additionally, there is a significant gap in the community's understanding of the environmental  impacts.  To  date,  community  consultation  efforts  have  been infrequent  and  limited,  with  project  information  largely  disseminated  among elites  in  Dili.  Future  consultations  should  prioritize  capturing  the  genuine attitudes  and  opinions  of  local  residents,  without  being  filtered  through  or accompanied by village and district officials.

- Resettlement  Action  Plan:  When  it  is  known  the  Project  will  proceed  and reasonably  final  site  layout  drawings  exist,  including  all  infrastructure  and  the inland residential site, it will be necessary to arrive at a determination of how many and  which  household  residences,  businesses,  and  other  structures  will  be physically relocated for the needs of the Project and/or the safety of occupants/users; and how many households will experience economic displacement.
- Census and Household Socioeconomic Survey: This will be the major input to the Resettlement  Action  Plan  (RAP)  which  will  detail  all  procedures  for  property valuation,  property  compensation  negotiation  and  pay-out,  other  forms  of compensation;  resettlement  procedures;  livelihood  restoration  programs;  and follow-up monitoring programs.
- Hydrogeological Investigation: Given the significant groundwater requirements, a detailed hydrogeological study is necessary to demonstrate the sustainability of groundwater abstraction at the proposed scale, ensuring sufficient and responsible resource management.
- Site  Selection  Review:  The  site  selection  process  should  be  comprehensively documented, illustrating  how  biodiversity  considerations  have  been  integrated into siting decisions to avoid adverse ecological impacts.
- Full ESIA: Expand the Preliminary ESIA into a full Environmental and Social Impact Assessment,  ensuring  compliance  with  the  requirements  of  IFC  Performance Standards.
